What is Nicotine?

Nicotine is a fast-acting drug found predominatly in tobacco, but can be found in very small amounts within tomatos, potatos, eggplants, and green peppers. Nicotine alkaloids may also be located in the leaves of a coca plant.


Nicotine is not a carcinogen, however, the drug is highly addictive. People who smoke tobacco find it hard to quit because they are addicted to the Nicotine found in tobacco. Nicotine can reach the brain within 15 seconds if it the drug is being inhaled. If nicotine was not in tobacco, the amount of poeple who smoke would drop drastically.

Immediate effects of Nicotine

Nicotine can cause irritation and a burning sensation in the mouth and throat,increased production of saliva, nausea, abdominal pain, vomiting, and diarrhea immediatly after inhaled from smoked tobacco.


Long Term effects of Nicotine

When nicotine is inhaled from smoked tobacco for a long period of time, the consumer may experience the following symptoms:

  • Increased risk of Heart Disease and Lung Cancer.
  • Hair Loss
  • Skin Irritation
  • Elevated Blood Pressure
  • Irregular Heartbeat
  • Insulin Resistance
  • And Gastrointenstinal issues.

What is Carbon Monoxide?

SILENT, but DEADLY.

Carbon monoxide is an oderless, colorless gas that can kill you.

The symptoms.

The most common symptoms of carbon monoxide poisining are:
  1. Headache
  2. Dizziness
  3. Weakness
  4. Stomach Ache
  5. Vomiting
  6. Chest Pain

    7. And Confusion.
    If you breathe in a lot of carbon monoxide, you may pass out or die.

Where is Carbon Monoxide?

Carbon monoxide, or "CO", can be found in fumes produced when fuel is burned in vehicles, small engines, gas stoves, lanterns, grills, fireplaces, gas ranges, and furnaces.

Who is at risk of Carbon Monoxide Poisining?

Short answer: Everyone
Long answer: Everyone, but there are things that make someone at a higher risk of getting CO Poisining.

  • Infants
  • The Elderly
  • People with chronic heart disease
  • Someone with Anemia

    • And people with breathing problems.

What happens when Lead fumes are inhaled?
When lead enters the respiratory system, it is released into the blood and distributed throughout the body. Over 90% of the lead accumulates in bones.

The symptoms of inhaling lead.

The symptoms can be divded into 6 categories, however, im only going to be covering 3 of them.

Neurological Effects of Lead Fumes.

  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Fatigue / Irritability
  • Impaired concentration
  • Hearing loss
  • Wrist / Foot drop
  • Seizures

    • And Encephalopathy

Gastrointestinal Effects of Lead Fumes.

  • Nausea
  • Dyspepsia
  • Constipation
  • Colic

Other Effects of Lead Fumes.
